The Great Gatsby

Turn simple ideas into powerful, elegant sentences

Your Mission

Bad writers write short. Great writers write specific. Your job is to take a boring, flat sentence and expand it — adding details about who, what, where, when, why, and how — until it does real work.

The 3-Level System
Level 1 — Beginner

Add one detail. Make the sentence longer by answering ONE question (where? when? why?).

Level 2 — Developing

Add two or more details. Use a comma, semicolon, or connecting word (although, because, while).

Level 3 — Advanced

Build a complex sentence with vivid description, figurative language, or a contrast that reveals character or theme.

Watch It Happen

Simple sentence:

Gatsby looked at the light.

Level 1 — Added WHERE:

Gatsby stood at the end of his dock and looked at the green light.

Level 2 — Added WHY + EMOTION:

Gatsby stood alone at the edge of his dock, reaching toward the green light across the bay, as if the distance between them might disappear if he wished hard enough.

Level 3 — Theme + Figurative Language:

Night after night, Gatsby stood at the trembling edge of his dock, arms outstretched toward Daisy's green light — not merely a beacon across the water, but the embodiment of every dream he had sacrificed his identity to possess.
The Steps
  1. Read the simple sentence carefully.
  2. Ask yourself: Who? What? Where? When? Why? How?
  3. Choose at least ONE detail from the story to add.
  4. Use a connecting word or punctuation to attach it.
  5. Read it out loud. Does it sound like real writing? If not, revise.
  6. Submit and get feedback from your AI writing coach.

Figurative Language to Steal

Fitzgerald uses these techniques. You should too.

"...a fantastic farm where ashes grow like wheat into ridges and hills and grotesque gardens."

Simile — comparing two unlike things using "like" or "as." The Valley of Ashes is like a farm — but instead of life, it grows waste.

The eyes of Doctor T.J. Eckleburg — enormous, unblinking — watch over a world they cannot save.

Symbol — an object that represents a larger idea. Eckleburg's eyes = a forgotten God watching a corrupt world.

The green light burns at the end of Daisy's dock — small, distant, unreachable.

Symbol + Contrast — the light is tiny but carries enormous emotional weight. Size vs. meaning.
